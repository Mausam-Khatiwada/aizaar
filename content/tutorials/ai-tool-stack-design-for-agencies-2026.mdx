## QuickAnswer
Most agencies lose money with AI not because tools are expensive, but because their stack is fragmented. A winning setup uses one orchestration layer, one research layer, one generation layer, and one QA/governance layer with clear owner responsibilities.

## Why most agency AI stacks fail
The common pattern is random tool adoption by team preference:
- Designer uses one image model
- Copywriter uses another writing model
- Strategy lead uses a third research model
- PM has no repeatable QA checklist

Output quality may look good in week one, but by month two:
- delivery quality becomes inconsistent
- revision rounds increase
- client trust drops
- margins shrink

## The four-layer architecture that scales

### 1. Discovery and research layer
Use one source-of-truth research flow:
- [Perplexity AI](/tools/perplexity-ai) for source-linked exploration
- [NotebookLM](/tools/notebooklm) for structured brief digestion

Deliverable: strategy brief with citations and assumptions.

### 2. Generation layer
Pick category-specific leaders, not a single "do everything" tool:
- Visuals: [Midjourney](/tools/midjourney), [Adobe Firefly](/tools/adobe-firefly)
- Video: [Runway ML](/tools/runway-ml), [Sora](/tools/sora)
- Voice: [ElevenLabs](/tools/elevenlabs)
- Long-form writing: [Claude](/tools/claude), [ChatGPT](/tools/chatgpt)

Deliverable: first-draft assets by channel.

### 3. Production and implementation layer
This is where many teams underinvest.
- [Cursor](/tools/cursor) for implementation velocity
- [GitHub Copilot](/tools/github-copilot) for in-editor acceleration
- [Notion AI](/tools/notion-ai) for handoff and SOP templating

Deliverable: production-ready launch package.

### 4. QA and governance layer
Every output should pass:
1. factual risk check
2. brand tone check
3. legal and licensing check
4. final human sign-off

Deliverable: approved asset with audit trail.

## Role mapping: who owns what
- Strategy lead: prompts, direction, final narrative
- Specialist operator: draft generation and iteration
- QA lead: verification and governance
- Technical owner: automations, API and integration stability

Do not let ownership blur. Ambiguity kills delivery speed.

## Margin model example (simple)
If AI cuts raw production time by 30% but revisions increase by 20%, your net gain is weak.

Track:
- hours saved per deliverable
- revision count per deliverable
- cycle time from brief to approval
- margin per client before vs after AI workflow

This is the dashboard that matters. Not vanity "tokens used".

## South Asian agency context: practical notes
- keep bilingual prompt packs for local campaigns
- maintain country-specific compliance and claim checklists
- avoid direct literal translation for ad tone; use transcreation
- create festival/event reusable templates for repeat demand

## 30-day rollout plan
- Week 1: audit current stack and workflows
- Week 2: choose core tools by category and retire overlaps
- Week 3: define QA checklist and sign-off process
- Week 4: ship one client project end-to-end with full instrumentation

## FAQs
### Should agencies standardize on one model provider?
Usually no. Standardize process first, then choose best-in-class tools by task.

### How many core tools are too many?
For most teams, 6-10 core tools is enough. Beyond that, coordination overhead rises quickly.

### How do we reduce hallucination risk in client work?
Use citation-first research and mandatory QA checks before delivery.

### Is this only for large agencies?
No. Small teams benefit even more because mistakes are costlier.

### What is the first tool to remove if stack is messy?
Remove duplicate tools solving the same task with no measurable quality advantage.
